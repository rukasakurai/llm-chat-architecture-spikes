# The Accelerating Transition to Renewable Energy: Drivers, Barriers, and Pathways to a Net-Zero Grid

**Report Series:** Energy Systems Analysis, Vol. 7
**Publication Date:** March 2024
**Authors:** Dr. Amara Diallo, Dr. Lena Hoffmann, Prof. Rajesh Kumar
**Institution:** Global Energy Research Consortium (GERC)

---

## Executive Summary

The global electricity system is undergoing its most significant structural transformation since electrification began in the late nineteenth century. Over the past decade, the levelised cost of electricity (LCOE) from utility-scale solar photovoltaic (PV) has fallen by more than 90 percent, and onshore wind by more than 70 percent, making renewables the cheapest source of new electricity generation in most markets worldwide. Yet despite this extraordinary cost reduction, the transition to a net-zero power sector faces persistent structural barriers: grid integration challenges, inadequate transmission infrastructure, permitting bottlenecks, financing constraints in emerging economies, and the complex politics of stranded-asset retirement.

This report synthesises the latest empirical evidence on the pace and character of the energy transition, identifies the principal drivers accelerating deployment, analyses the barriers that continue to slow progress, and evaluates emerging pathways toward a fully decarbonised electricity grid. The analysis draws on project-level deployment data, macroeconomic modelling, and policy case studies from thirty-five countries.

Key findings:

1. Renewable energy now accounts for approximately 30 percent of global electricity generation, up from 19 percent in 2010.
2. Solar and wind additions in 2023 reached a record 507 GW, exceeding the capacity of the entire United States coal fleet.
3. Battery storage deployment is accelerating rapidly but remains insufficient to address multi-day and seasonal variability at the system level.
4. Grid-scale hydrogen and long-duration storage are unlikely to be cost-competitive before 2035 in most markets.
5. Without substantial acceleration of transmission and distribution network investment, renewable deployment will be curtailment-constrained in major markets including the EU, the United States, and India.
6. The financing gap for clean energy in emerging markets and developing economies (EMDEs) reached an estimated USD 570 billion in 2023.
7. Fossil fuel phase-out will require active industrial policy: market signals alone are insufficient to manage a just transition.

## 1. Introduction

The 2015 Paris Agreement set the political framework for global decarbonisation, establishing the goal of limiting warming to 1.5 °C above pre-industrial levels and calling on countries to achieve net-zero greenhouse gas emissions in the second half of the century. The electricity sector, responsible for approximately 25 percent of global greenhouse gas emissions, is widely regarded as both the most tractable and the most strategically important sector to decarbonise first. Unlike heat, transport, or industry — where zero-carbon alternatives remain expensive or technically immature — clean electricity can be produced at or below the cost of fossil generation and can serve as the backbone of economy-wide decarbonisation through electrification.

The past decade has validated the broad outlines of this strategy. Solar PV and wind have scaled from niche technologies to dominant sources of new capacity addition. Battery costs have fallen nearly as steeply as solar, enabling the emergence of a utility-scale storage industry. Electric vehicles are gaining market share rapidly, and heat pumps are displacing gas boilers in an increasing number of markets.

Yet the transition remains far too slow to meet the 1.5 °C target. The International Energy Agency's (IEA) Net Zero by 2050 scenario requires that clean electricity capacity triple by 2030 and that coal power be largely eliminated from the power mix in advanced economies within a decade. On current trajectories, the world will fall well short of these milestones.

This report addresses four interconnected questions:

1. What is driving the accelerating deployment of renewable energy?
2. What structural barriers continue to impede faster progress?
3. What are the most promising technological and policy pathways to a net-zero grid?
4. What are the equity and justice dimensions of the transition, particularly for workers in fossil fuel industries and for energy access in the developing world?

## 2. The Economics of the Energy Transition

### 2.1 Cost Trajectories

The single most important driver of the renewable energy transition is cost. Between 2010 and 2023, the global weighted-average LCOE of utility-scale solar PV fell from USD 0.38/kWh to USD 0.044/kWh — a 88 percent reduction. Onshore wind fell from USD 0.089/kWh to USD 0.033/kWh over the same period. Both technologies are now cheaper than any new-build fossil fuel option in most markets, and in the sunniest and windiest locations they undercut even the marginal operating costs of existing gas plants.

These cost reductions are driven by a combination of learning-by-doing (each doubling of cumulative installed capacity reduces costs by a predictable percentage), scale economies in manufacturing, supply chain globalisation, and incremental engineering improvements. For solar PV, the learning rate has been approximately 24 percent — meaning every doubling of capacity reduces costs by 24 percent. For wind, the rate is approximately 12 percent.

The implications of sustained learning curves are profound. Even without policy support, solar and wind economics will continue to improve as deployment scales. The GERC baseline scenario projects solar LCOE reaching USD 0.020/kWh by 2030 and USD 0.015/kWh by 2035 in high-irradiance markets.

### 2.2 The Value Deflation Problem

The falling cost of generation is, however, only part of the economic story. As variable renewable energy (VRE) penetration increases, its market value declines — a phenomenon known as value deflation or the cannibalisation effect. When solar generation peaks at midday, power prices collapse; when wind is plentiful, prices fall across all hours of high wind output.

Analysis of electricity market data from Germany, California, and South Australia — three markets with high VRE penetration — shows that solar's capture price (the time-weighted average price at which solar actually sells electricity) has fallen to 50–70 percent of the baseload price in these markets, compared with near 100 percent at low penetration levels. As penetration increases further, this discount will widen.

Value deflation does not undermine the case for renewables, but it does reshape the economics of the transition. It increases the value of storage, grid interconnection, and demand flexibility. It also means that the system-level cost of decarbonisation includes not just generation costs but also balancing, storage, and network costs — the "integration costs" that are sometimes absent from simple LCOE comparisons.

### 2.3 Battery Storage Economics

Lithium-ion battery storage has followed a cost trajectory almost as steep as solar PV. Pack-level costs fell from over USD 1,200/kWh in 2010 to approximately USD 130/kWh in 2023, a decline of nearly 90 percent. Grid-scale battery systems (4-hour duration) now have an LCOE of approximately USD 0.08–0.12/kWh for energy arbitrage applications in favourable markets.

This has enabled a rapid expansion of grid-scale battery deployment: global utility-scale battery additions reached 45 GWh in 2023, up from less than 1 GWh in 2015. The United States, China, and Australia lead deployment. Battery systems are primarily deployed for short-duration applications: frequency response, peak shaving, and 2–4 hour energy arbitrage.

However, batteries are not a solution to multi-day variability or seasonal imbalances. A typical temperate-climate grid faces "dark doldrums" periods — cloudy and windless periods lasting several days to weeks — during which neither solar nor wind generates adequately. Addressing these periods requires either significant overcapacity of generation and storage, long-duration storage technologies, firm low-carbon generation (nuclear, geothermal, hydro), or regional transmission to exploit geographic diversity.

## 3. Drivers of Accelerating Deployment

### 3.1 Policy Support

Despite the dramatic cost reductions of the past decade, policy support has been instrumental in driving deployment. Feed-in tariffs (FITs), renewable portfolio standards (RPS), tax credits, and competitive auctions have all played important roles in different markets and different phases of technology development.

The United States Inflation Reduction Act (IRA), passed in August 2022, represents the largest climate investment in American history: USD 369 billion in clean energy tax credits and incentives over ten years. The IRA's investment tax credits (ITC) and production tax credits (PTC) for solar, wind, storage, and clean hydrogen are technology-neutral and long-duration (ten years), providing a stable investment signal. Early evidence suggests the IRA is catalysing a manufacturing renaissance: more than USD 100 billion in announced clean energy manufacturing investments in the United States in the eighteen months after enactment.

The European Union's REPowerEU plan, adopted in response to the energy security crisis triggered by Russia's invasion of Ukraine, raised the EU's 2030 renewable energy target to 42.5 percent of final energy consumption and introduced a series of measures to accelerate permitting, procurement, and grid investment. The EU's Fit for 55 package further reinforces these targets through emissions trading, carbon border adjustment, and efficiency standards.

China's industrial policy has been decisive in driving global solar and battery manufacturing cost reductions. Chinese manufacturers account for more than 85 percent of global solar module production and a similar share of battery cell manufacturing. State-directed investment in manufacturing capacity, upstream raw materials, and supply chain integration has produced scale economies that no other country has yet matched.

### 3.2 Corporate Procurement

Corporate power purchase agreements (PPAs) have emerged as a major driver of renewable deployment, particularly in markets where policy support is limited or uncertain. Corporates in technology, retail, and manufacturing sectors have made voluntary commitments to 100 percent renewable electricity (often through the RE100 initiative) and are procuring directly from renewable developers through long-term bilateral contracts.

Global corporate PPA volumes reached approximately 50 GW in 2023, up from under 5 GW in 2015. The United States accounts for approximately 40 percent of global volumes, followed by Europe (30 percent) and the Asia-Pacific region (20 percent). Corporate PPAs provide revenue certainty for developers, often enabling projects to reach financial close without government support.

### 3.3 Energy Security Motivations

The energy crisis of 2021–2022, driven first by post-COVID commodity demand recovery and then by the disruption of Russian gas supplies to Europe, has fundamentally shifted the political economy of the energy transition in many markets. Policymakers who previously viewed the energy transition primarily through a climate lens now emphasise energy security, supply chain resilience, and industrial competitiveness.

This has accelerated domestic manufacturing ambitions in the EU and United States and has driven significant diversification of natural gas import infrastructure (LNG terminals) alongside accelerated renewables deployment. In some markets, the crisis has temporarily reversed progress — German coal plants that had been mothballed were restarted — but the medium-term effect has been to accelerate the transition, as the volatility of fossil fuel prices underscored the value of zero-marginal-cost renewables.

## 4. Structural Barriers to the Transition

### 4.1 Grid Infrastructure and Transmission Constraints

The most binding constraint on renewable deployment in many advanced economies is not the cost of generation but the availability of grid connection and transmission capacity to move electricity from where it is generated (often remote, windy, or sunny areas) to where it is consumed (urban centres and industrial clusters).

In the United States, the interconnection queue — the list of projects waiting for grid connection approval — had grown to over 2,600 GW by end of 2023, with the average project waiting more than five years for approval. The Federal Energy Regulatory Commission (FERC) has adopted new rules (Order 2023) to reform the interconnection process, but grid operators face a structural capacity problem: the queue has grown faster than it can be processed.

In Europe, the pace of grid expansion has lagged behind renewable deployment. Key transmission bottlenecks exist between the North Sea (where offshore wind is rapidly scaling) and demand centres in central and southern Europe; between Iberian solar resources and northern markets; and between Nordic hydro and continental Europe. The EU has identified 166 Projects of Common Interest (PCIs) — strategic cross-border transmission lines — but construction is proceeding slowly, hampered by permitting complexity, land access disputes, and cross-border regulatory coordination.

Offshore wind, which is scaling rapidly in Europe, the United States, and increasingly Asia, faces particular grid challenges. Offshore projects require costly underwater cable infrastructure, and the regulatory and permitting regime for offshore cables is complex and jurisdiction-specific. Several major offshore wind projects in the United States and United Kingdom have faced financial stress partly due to unexpected grid connection costs.

### 4.2 Permitting and Siting

Permitting and siting remain significant bottlenecks in many countries, particularly for large onshore wind and high-voltage transmission projects. Typical permitting timelines for onshore wind in EU member states range from 4 to 9 years; for high-voltage transmission, timelines of 10–15 years are common.

Environmental impact assessment requirements, public consultation processes, legal challenge risks, and fragmented regulatory competences (particularly in federal states) all contribute to delay. The EU has introduced reforms through the Renewable Energy Directive (RED III) to streamline permitting, including the designation of "Renewables Acceleration Areas" where simplified permitting applies and time limits for approvals. Several member states are implementing these reforms, but cultural and administrative change is slow.

In the United States, the National Environmental Policy Act (NEPA) review process has been a source of significant delay for both generation and transmission projects. The Fiscal Responsibility Act of 2023 introduced some streamlining measures, but fundamental reform of federal permitting remains politically contested.

### 4.3 Financing Constraints in Emerging Markets

While the economics of renewable energy are compelling in advanced economies with access to deep capital markets, low interest rates, and strong institutional frameworks, the situation in many emerging markets and developing economies (EMDEs) is profoundly different. The cost of capital for a solar project in sub-Saharan Africa or South Asia can be two to three times higher than in Germany or the United States, partly or entirely negating the technology cost advantage.

This financing gap has structural causes: currency risk (most renewable equipment is priced in USD or EUR), political and regulatory risk, shallow domestic capital markets, limited credit history for project finance, and the absence of de-risking mechanisms (guarantees, first-loss instruments, concessional finance). The GERC estimates that bridging the clean energy financing gap in EMDEs would require USD 1 trillion in additional annual finance by 2030 — roughly double current flows.

Multilateral development banks (MDBs) and development finance institutions (DFIs) play a critical de-risking role, but their balance sheets are insufficient to close the gap at scale. The reform of MDB capital adequacy frameworks — allowing them to take more risk and leverage private finance more aggressively — is a major focus of international climate finance diplomacy.

### 4.4 Social Acceptance and Just Transition

Large-scale renewable deployment requires significant land use: a solar farm with 1 GW of capacity requires roughly 5,000–10,000 hectares; an offshore wind farm of similar capacity requires 200–400 km² of sea area. The social acceptance of these footprints is not guaranteed.

Community opposition to wind turbines (on aesthetic, noise, and shadow-flicker grounds) has been a significant constraint on onshore wind deployment in parts of Europe and North America. Agricultural communities have expressed concerns about the "paving over" of productive farmland with solar panels. Transmission lines face opposition from rural landowners and local governments.

Proponents of the energy transition argue that community benefit models — direct revenue sharing with local communities, co-ownership structures, and local employment guarantees — can substantially improve social acceptance. Evidence from Denmark, Scotland, and several German Länder supports this view. However, benefit models require regulatory enablement and cannot wholly overcome deep-seated opposition in some communities.

The just transition dimension — the management of employment and economic impacts on workers and communities dependent on fossil fuel industries — is equally important. Global coal employment is approximately 7 million workers; oil and gas employment is approximately 12 million. While net employment effects of the transition are likely positive, the geographic concentration of fossil fuel employment in specific communities (Appalachian coal country in the United States, the Ruhr in Germany, coal regions in Poland and India) means that the costs and benefits are distributed very unevenly.

## 5. Pathways to a Net-Zero Grid

### 5.1 The Role of Wind and Solar

Wind and solar will be the backbone of any credible net-zero electricity system. The IEA's NZE scenario projects that solar and wind together provide 68 percent of global electricity generation by 2030 (up from approximately 13 percent in 2022) and nearly 90 percent by 2050. Achieving this requires annual additions of 600–800 GW/year of solar and 350–400 GW/year of wind through 2030.

The manufacturing and supply chain capacity to achieve these additions largely exists or is being built out. The more binding constraints are grid infrastructure, permitting, and financing — as described above. Policy reform in these areas is therefore the priority for accelerating the transition.

Agrivoltaics — the co-location of solar panels with agricultural production — has emerged as a promising approach to reducing land-use conflicts. Studies in Europe and Japan show that carefully designed agrivoltaic installations can maintain 70–90 percent of crop yields while generating substantial solar electricity, reducing evapotranspiration (beneficial in water-stressed regions), and providing a second income stream for farmers.

Offshore wind is scaling rapidly and offers several advantages over onshore: higher and more consistent wind speeds (particularly in floating offshore applications), absence of land-use conflicts, and access to large coastal demand centres. However, offshore wind is significantly more expensive than onshore (LCOE approximately USD 0.08–0.12/kWh in 2023), and supply chains for the largest turbines (15–20 MW class) are still being scaled.

### 5.2 Long-Duration Energy Storage

Short-duration battery storage (2–4 hours) is well-suited to handling diurnal variability (day-night cycles), but addressing multi-day and seasonal variability requires long-duration storage (LDES) technologies. Several LDES approaches are under development:

**Pumped hydro storage (PHS)** is the dominant existing LDES technology, accounting for more than 90 percent of global storage capacity. PHS is mature, low-cost (USD 0.05–0.10/kWh at long durations), and highly scalable, but is geographically constrained to suitable terrain. The global pipeline of planned PHS projects could add 100–150 GW of capacity by 2030.

**Green hydrogen** produced by electrolysis using surplus renewable electricity can be stored and reconverted to electricity via fuel cells or gas turbines. Hydrogen offers essentially unlimited storage duration and is a leading option for seasonal storage. However, the round-trip efficiency of the power-to-hydrogen-to-power pathway is approximately 35–45 percent, compared with 85–90 percent for lithium-ion batteries. Green hydrogen electrolyser costs are falling rapidly but remain significantly above the levels needed for cost-competitive seasonal storage in most markets.

**Iron-air batteries** (developed by companies such as Form Energy) offer multi-day storage at estimated costs below USD 20/kWh of energy capacity, significantly lower than lithium-ion. Commercial-scale deployment is beginning in 2024–2025. Iron-air batteries have lower energy density than lithium-ion, making them more suitable for stationary grid storage than for transport applications.

**Flow batteries** (vanadium, iron-chromium, and others) decouple power and energy ratings, allowing very large energy capacities at relatively low marginal cost. Vanadium flow batteries are commercially deployed at grid scale in China; cost reductions and supply chain development are ongoing.

**Compressed air energy storage (CAES)** and **liquid air energy storage (LAES)** store energy in the form of compressed or liquefied air. Both technologies are at early commercial stages. CAES is geographically constrained to suitable geological formations (caverns, depleted gas reservoirs), while LAES is more flexible but has lower round-trip efficiency (~50–60 percent).

### 5.3 Nuclear Energy

Nuclear energy has historically been a major source of low-carbon electricity in many advanced economies (France at ~70 percent nuclear, South Korea at ~30 percent, the United States at ~20 percent). However, the nuclear fleet in advanced economies is ageing, and new nuclear construction has been plagued by cost overruns and delays.

The high-profile failures of the Hinkley Point C project in the United Kingdom (budget over USD 35 billion for 3.2 GW) and the Vogtle project in the United States (final cost over USD 35 billion for 2.2 GW) have raised serious questions about the economic viability of large light-water reactors in high-wage markets.

Small modular reactors (SMRs) have attracted significant policy attention as a potentially more cost-effective nuclear option. SMR designs aim for lower upfront capital costs through factory fabrication and modular construction. However, no SMR has yet reached commercial operation, and the cost estimates for first-of-a-kind units remain highly uncertain. The GERC baseline scenario does not rely on significant SMR deployment before 2040.

In the near term, the most cost-effective nuclear contribution is the extension of the operating lifetimes of existing reactors. The United States has successfully extended reactor lifetimes to 60 and 80 years; several European countries are following suit.

### 5.4 Demand Flexibility and Smart Grid Technologies

Demand flexibility — the ability of electricity consumers to shift or reduce consumption in response to grid conditions and price signals — is a low-cost resource that can significantly reduce the need for peaking capacity and storage. Key demand flexibility resources include:

**Electric vehicles (EVs):** The global EV fleet reached 40 million vehicles in 2023. With smart charging, EV batteries can absorb surplus renewable generation (charging during periods of low prices/high renewable output) and, in vehicle-to-grid (V2G) configurations, return stored energy to the grid during periods of scarcity. IEA analysis suggests a fully V2G-enabled global EV fleet could provide 14 TWh of storage capacity by 2030 — more than current global battery storage capacity.

**Heat pumps and smart water heaters:** Space heating and water heating loads are well-suited to demand response: thermal mass provides short-term flexibility, and water tanks provide effective storage. A smart water heater can shift its operation by several hours with minimal comfort impact. With 100 million smart heat pumps deployed, flexible heating could provide hundreds of GW of demand response capacity.

**Industrial demand response:** Energy-intensive industries (aluminium smelting, chlor-alkali production, data centres, desalination) can curtail or shift significant loads in response to price signals or grid conditions. These industries are often already sophisticated electricity market participants.

Enabling demand flexibility at scale requires smart meters with real-time pricing, aggregation platforms, and regulatory frameworks that allow aggregators to participate in wholesale and balancing markets. Retail electricity market design — often set by state or local regulators in federal systems — is a critical but often underappreciated lever for the energy transition.

## 6. Regional Case Studies

### 6.1 The European Union: Ambition and Implementation Gap

The EU has set among the world's most ambitious renewable energy targets — 42.5 percent of final energy consumption from renewables by 2030 — and has backed these with a comprehensive policy framework. However, implementation is constrained by fragmented member state permitting regimes, grid investment gaps, and the complexity of coordinating 27 national energy systems.

In 2023, the EU added approximately 56 GW of solar and 17 GW of wind — a record for both technologies. Solar deployment is accelerating rapidly, particularly on rooftops; Germany added over 14 GW of solar in 2023, a new national record. However, wind deployment remains below the pace required to meet 2030 targets, primarily due to permitting delays.

The EU's transmission network faces a significant investment requirement: the European Network of Transmission System Operators for Electricity (ENTSO-E) estimates EUR 100 billion of investment is needed by 2030 to handle projected renewable deployment. Planning and permitting of new transmission lines is progressing but is running behind the construction pace needed.

### 6.2 The United States: IRA Impact and Grid Challenges

The Inflation Reduction Act has transformed the U.S. clean energy investment landscape. More than USD 300 billion in clean energy manufacturing investments have been announced since the IRA's passage, spanning solar module production, battery gigafactories, wind turbine component manufacturing, and EV supply chains. Utility-scale solar additions reached a record 32 GW in 2023.

However, the interconnection queue bottleneck is a severe constraint. FERC's Order 2023 reforms aim to reduce queue times, but stakeholders expect the queue to take several years to clear. In the near term, projects that have already secured grid positions and PPAs are advancing rapidly; the constraint is on new projects entering the pipeline.

The United States also faces significant challenges in building interstate high-voltage transmission lines. The fragmented regulatory landscape — with federal, state, and local authorities all having a role — has historically made siting new transmission lines extremely difficult. Recent legislative changes have strengthened FERC's transmission siting authority, but implementation will take time.

### 6.3 India: Ambition, Scale, and Financing

India has set a target of 500 GW of non-fossil electricity capacity by 2030 and has made substantial progress: renewable capacity reached approximately 185 GW by end of 2023, with solar the dominant contributor. India's solar irradiance and land availability make it one of the world's most naturally advantaged countries for solar deployment.

However, India faces substantial challenges:

- **Electricity distribution company (DISCOM) financial health:** Many state-owned distribution companies are financially distressed, with accumulated losses limiting their ability to sign long-term PPAs and invest in grid modernisation.
- **Grid infrastructure:** Transmission and distribution networks require massive investment to absorb the projected scale of renewable generation.
- **Financing cost:** The cost of debt for infrastructure projects in India (8–10 percent) is significantly higher than in advanced economies, affecting project economics.
- **Manufacturing:** India is seeking to develop domestic solar manufacturing to reduce import dependence on China, but the domestic supply chain is still developing.

### 6.4 Sub-Saharan Africa: Energy Access and the Leapfrog Opportunity

Sub-Saharan Africa faces a different set of challenges: approximately 600 million people lack access to electricity, and economic growth is constrained by unreliable power supply. The continent has extraordinary renewable resources — some of the world's highest solar irradiance and significant wind, hydro, and geothermal potential — but utilisation is minimal.

The key challenge is not technology cost but financing and institutional capacity. MDB reform, blended finance mechanisms (combining concessional public finance with private capital), and the development of regional power pools (such as the Eastern Africa Power Pool and the Southern African Power Pool) are the most important levers.

Off-grid solar (solar home systems and mini-grids) has made remarkable progress in recent years, with costs falling dramatically and pay-as-you-go business models making solar accessible to low-income households. However, off-grid solar alone cannot meet the energy needs of industrial development; expanding grid infrastructure and utility-scale generation remains essential.

## 7. Conclusions and Recommendations

### 7.1 Key Findings

The renewable energy transition is accelerating dramatically, driven by extraordinary cost reductions in solar, wind, and battery storage. However, the pace remains insufficient to achieve the 1.5 °C climate target, and the gap between current trajectories and required progress is concentrated in specific structural bottlenecks: grid infrastructure, permitting, and EMDE financing.

The technology case for a net-zero grid is now essentially settled. Solar and wind are the cheapest forms of new electricity generation in most of the world. The challenge is not whether to transition but how to do so at the required speed and in a manner consistent with energy security, equity, and industrial competitiveness.

### 7.2 Priority Recommendations

**For governments in advanced economies:**

1. **Accelerate permitting reform** for both generation and transmission, including the designation of pre-approved development zones, streamlined environmental review, and time-limited approval processes.
2. **Prioritise transmission investment** as the single most important enabling infrastructure for the energy transition. This requires new financing mechanisms (regulated returns, green bonds, public financing) and regulatory reform to improve interstate/cross-border coordination.
3. **Implement long-duration storage policy** frameworks, including capacity markets or contracts for difference that provide revenue certainty for LDES projects with 10–15 year capital recovery horizons.
4. **Reform retail electricity markets** to enable demand flexibility, including real-time pricing, smart meter mandates, and aggregator market access.
5. **Fund just transition programmes** for fossil fuel communities at a scale commensurate with the challenge, including worker retraining, economic diversification, and community investment.

**For multilateral institutions:**

1. **Reform MDB capital adequacy frameworks** to unlock additional balance sheet capacity for clean energy lending in EMDEs.
2. **Scale blended finance mechanisms** to de-risk private investment in EMDE clean energy projects.
3. **Strengthen regional power pool institutions** in sub-Saharan Africa, South and Southeast Asia, and Latin America to facilitate cross-border clean energy trade.
4. **Support domestic clean energy manufacturing** in EMDEs through technology transfer, skills development, and supply chain investment.

**For the private sector:**

1. **Accelerate corporate PPA commitments** and support policy reform in markets where procurement frameworks are inadequate.
2. **Invest in supply chain diversification** to reduce concentration risk in solar and battery manufacturing.
3. **Pilot community benefit models** at scale to improve social acceptance of large renewable projects.

### 7.3 Looking Ahead

The 2020s will be the decisive decade for the energy transition. Renewable costs will continue to fall; battery storage will scale; the electrification of transport and heating will gather pace. The question is whether the policy, regulatory, and financial systems can be reformed quickly enough to enable deployment at the scale and speed the climate requires.

The stakes could not be higher. Energy systems are long-lived infrastructure with lock-in periods of 20–40 years. Decisions made in this decade — about which power plants are built, which transmission lines are permitted, which industrial facilities are decarbonised — will shape the trajectory of global emissions for a generation. The evidence reviewed in this report suggests that the transition is possible and increasingly economically attractive. Whether it happens fast enough is a question of political will, institutional capacity, and international cooperation.

## 8. The Role of Digitalisation and Artificial Intelligence

### 8.1 Smart Grid Technologies

The energy transition is inseparable from the digital transformation of electricity networks. Smart grid technologies — advanced metering infrastructure, real-time monitoring, automated switching, and distributed control systems — are the foundational layer that enables the integration of high shares of variable renewable energy, distributed generation, and flexible demand.

Advanced metering infrastructure (AMI) has been deployed to more than 50 percent of residential customers in the United States and most of the EU, providing granular consumption data and enabling dynamic pricing. However, the value of smart meters depends critically on the tariff structures and consumer-facing applications that make use of the data they generate. In most markets, smart meters have been installed without the retail market reforms needed to realise their potential.

Distribution network automation — automated fault detection, isolation, and restoration (FDIR) — reduces the duration and scale of power outages and enables higher penetration of distributed energy resources (DERs: rooftop solar, home batteries, EVs) by maintaining network stability. Grid-edge intelligence, in which distribution network operators manage thousands of small devices rather than a handful of large generators, requires investment in operational technology, cybersecurity, and workforce capabilities.

### 8.2 AI Applications in Energy Systems

Artificial intelligence and machine learning are finding applications across the energy value chain:

**Renewable energy forecasting:** Machine learning models for solar irradiance and wind speed prediction have substantially improved the accuracy of generation forecasts over 1–48 hour horizons. Accurate forecasting reduces balancing costs, enables more efficient transmission scheduling, and improves the economic performance of renewable assets.

**Grid operations:** AI-assisted energy management systems can optimise power flows across transmission networks in near-real-time, reducing transmission losses and bottlenecks. Google's DeepMind demonstrated a 40 percent reduction in data centre cooling costs using reinforcement learning; similar approaches are being applied to substation and network control.

**Demand forecasting and flexibility dispatch:** AI models can predict demand at sub-hourly resolution at the distribution level, enabling more accurate capacity planning and more efficient dispatch of flexible resources. Aggregation platforms for demand response use machine learning to optimise the dispatch of thousands of smart thermostats, water heaters, and EV chargers in response to grid signals.

**Predictive maintenance:** Machine learning applied to sensor data from wind turbines, solar inverters, and transmission infrastructure can identify failure precursors, enabling maintenance before failures occur. Studies suggest predictive maintenance can reduce unplanned downtime by 30–50 percent and maintenance costs by 15–25 percent.

**Long-term planning:** AI and optimisation tools are being used to improve the quality and speed of long-term energy system planning, including transmission network investment planning, technology portfolio optimisation, and climate-risk stress testing.

The integration of AI into energy systems raises questions about cybersecurity, data governance, model explainability, and the concentration of analytical capability in a small number of technology providers. These issues are beginning to receive regulatory attention.

## 9. Clean Hydrogen: Potential, Progress, and Pitfalls

### 9.1 The Hydrogen Economy Premise

Hydrogen has attracted enormous policy attention as a potential carrier for decarbonised energy, particularly in applications where direct electrification is difficult or costly: long-distance shipping, aviation, iron and steel production, chemicals synthesis, and seasonal energy storage. The concept of a "hydrogen economy" — in which hydrogen produced from renewable electricity (green hydrogen) or from natural gas with carbon capture (blue hydrogen) displaces fossil fuels across multiple sectors — has driven billions in public and private investment.

Green hydrogen is produced by electrolysis: passing an electrical current through water to separate it into hydrogen and oxygen. The electrolyser capacity needed to produce meaningful quantities of green hydrogen at the scale required for industrial decarbonisation is enormous. The IEA estimates that achieving net zero by 2050 requires approximately 3,000 GW of electrolyser capacity globally — compared with approximately 0.7 GW of capacity operating in 2022.

### 9.2 Cost Reductions and Deployment Progress

Electrolyser costs have been falling rapidly, from approximately USD 1,200/kW for polymer electrolyte membrane (PEM) electrolysers in 2015 to approximately USD 400–700/kW in 2023, with further reductions expected as manufacturing scales. However, the cost of green hydrogen production (including electrolyser, electricity, water, and balance of plant) remains approximately USD 3–8/kg H₂ in most markets — significantly above the USD 1–2/kg at which it would be competitive with grey hydrogen (produced from natural gas without carbon capture) or blue hydrogen.

The cost of green hydrogen is sensitive primarily to the cost of renewable electricity (which constitutes 60–70 percent of production cost) and electrolyser utilisation rate. In locations with abundant low-cost renewable generation (Middle East, Chile, Australia, parts of North Africa), green hydrogen costs are approaching the lower end of the current range and are projected to reach USD 1–2/kg by 2030 under optimistic scenarios.

### 9.3 Infrastructure Challenges

The development of a hydrogen economy faces significant infrastructure challenges beyond production costs:

**Transport and distribution:** Hydrogen has very low volumetric energy density at ambient conditions, requiring either compression (at 350–700 bar), liquefaction (at –253 °C), or chemical conversion (to ammonia or liquid organic hydrogen carriers) for cost-effective transport. Each of these options involves energy losses and additional capital costs.

**End-use equipment:** Most end-use applications require either fuel cells (which have their own cost and durability challenges) or hydrogen-adapted combustion equipment. Retrofitting existing natural gas infrastructure for hydrogen involves significant technical and safety challenges.

**Safety:** Hydrogen is colourless, odourless, highly flammable, and has a wide flammability range. It can cause hydrogen embrittlement in steel pipelines and storage vessels. Safe handling requires specialised training, equipment, and codes and standards that are still being developed and harmonised internationally.

### 9.4 Realistic Role of Hydrogen in the Energy Transition

Most credible energy transition scenarios assign green hydrogen a significant but specialised role: primarily in hard-to-abate industrial processes (ammonia, methanol, direct reduced iron for steelmaking) and potentially in long-haul shipping and aviation (via synthetic fuels), rather than as a general-purpose energy carrier replacing electricity in heating, light-duty transport, or short-duration storage.

The "hydrogen for everything" narrative that characterised early hydrogen strategies in several countries has been tempered by sober analysis of round-trip efficiency losses and infrastructure costs. Direct electrification (with batteries, heat pumps, and electric motors) is generally more efficient and lower-cost than electrolysis + hydrogen wherever it is technically feasible.

## 10. Geopolitics of the Energy Transition

### 10.1 Supply Chain Concentration Risks

The rapid growth of clean energy technologies has created new supply chain concentration risks. China dominates the production of solar modules (>85%), battery cells (>75%), wind turbine components (>60%), and key mineral processing (cobalt, lithium, rare earth elements used in permanent magnets for wind turbines and EV motors). This concentration reflects China's sustained industrial policy investment over two decades and the cost advantages that have driven global technology deployment.

For advanced economies that have grown accustomed to importing fossil fuels from a diversified set of suppliers, the prospect of critical clean energy supply chains concentrated in a single country with which they have complex geopolitical relationships creates significant concern. The EU's Critical Raw Materials Act, the U.S. Inflation Reduction Act's domestic content requirements, and Japan's GX (Green Transformation) strategy all reflect a desire to build more diversified and domestically anchored clean energy supply chains.

However, diversification will take time and comes with costs. Restricting access to low-cost Chinese solar panels and batteries would increase the cost of the energy transition in the near term. Policymakers face a genuine tension between supply chain resilience and cost-effective decarbonisation.

### 10.2 Mineral Supply Security

The energy transition is highly material-intensive. A single 3 MW wind turbine requires approximately 335 kg of rare earth metals (for permanent magnets), 4.7 tonnes of copper, and significant quantities of steel and aluminium. Electric vehicle batteries require lithium, cobalt, nickel, and manganese. Grid-scale battery storage requires similar materials at vastly larger scale.

Ensuring adequate and responsible supply of these critical minerals is a major policy challenge. The Democratic Republic of Congo supplies approximately 70 percent of global cobalt production; the concentration of lithium mining in the "Lithium Triangle" of Argentina, Bolivia, and Chile; the concentration of rare earth processing in China — all create supply chain vulnerabilities.

International cooperation on critical minerals — through bodies like the Minerals Security Partnership and bilateral agreements — is accelerating. The mining and processing industry is responding to demand signals, but lead times for new mines (typically 10–17 years from discovery to production) mean that near-term supply tightness is a real risk as EV and battery storage deployment accelerates.

### 10.3 Energy Transition and Global Power Dynamics

The energy transition is reshaping global power dynamics in ways that are still unfolding. The relative importance of oil and gas exporters in global politics has been tied to the world's dependence on fossil fuels; as that dependence declines, the geopolitical weight of petrostates will shift.

However, this transition will take decades and is not linear. In the near term, Russian fossil fuel revenues remain significant despite sanctions. Gulf producers are using current hydrocarbon revenues to fund sovereign wealth fund investments in clean energy and economic diversification. Major oil and gas companies are making varied bets: some are investing heavily in renewables and hydrogen; others are doubling down on hydrocarbon production.

The rise of clean energy technology exports as a new form of strategic economic power is also reshaping geopolitics. China's dominance of solar, batteries, and EVs is analogous in some ways to Saudi Arabia's dominance of oil: a structural economic advantage in a commodity that the world needs. Managing the geopolitical dimensions of this shift is one of the defining challenges of the coming decade.

## Appendix C: Glossary

**CAES:** Compressed Air Energy Storage — a long-duration storage technology that stores energy as compressed air in underground caverns or purpose-built vessels.

**DER:** Distributed Energy Resource — a small-scale electricity generation or storage asset connected to the distribution network, such as rooftop solar, home batteries, or electric vehicles.

**FDIR:** Fault Detection, Isolation, and Restoration — automated distribution network switching technology that minimises the scope and duration of power outages.

**GW:** Gigawatt — a unit of power equal to one billion watts. Used to measure the nameplate capacity of power plants and energy storage systems.

**LAES:** Liquid Air Energy Storage — a technology that stores energy by liquefying air at cryogenic temperatures and re-expanding it through a turbine to generate electricity.

**LCOE:** Levelised Cost of Electricity — the average cost per unit of electricity over the lifetime of a generating asset, accounting for capital cost, operating cost, fuel cost, and capacity factor.

**LDES:** Long-Duration Energy Storage — storage technologies capable of delivering electricity for more than four hours, typically used to address multi-day or seasonal variability.

**PHS:** Pumped Hydro Storage — the most mature large-scale energy storage technology, which stores energy by pumping water from a lower reservoir to an upper reservoir and recovers it by releasing the water through turbines.

**PPA:** Power Purchase Agreement — a long-term contract between an electricity generator and a buyer (utility, corporation, or other entity) specifying the price and terms for electricity supply.

**SMR:** Small Modular Reactor — a nuclear reactor design with lower output (typically 20–300 MW) intended to achieve lower capital costs through factory fabrication and modular construction.

**TWh:** Terawatt-hour — a unit of energy equal to one trillion watt-hours. Used to measure annual electricity generation.

**VRE:** Variable Renewable Energy — renewable energy sources whose output varies with weather conditions, primarily solar PV and wind.

**WACC:** Weighted Average Cost of Capital — the blended cost of financing a project, accounting for the relative proportions and costs of debt and equity.

## Appendix D: Extended Analysis of Grid Integration Challenges

### D.1 The Duck Curve and Net Load Variability

One of the most well-documented challenges of high solar penetration is the "duck curve" — the characteristic shape of the net load curve (total demand minus solar generation) in a high-solar grid. In the middle of the day, solar generation suppresses net load to very low levels; in the evening, as solar generation falls and demand peaks, net load rises sharply, requiring rapid ramping of dispatchable generation.

The California Independent System Operator (CAISO) has tracked this phenomenon since the mid-2010s. By 2023, CAISO's minimum net load was frequently below 5 GW, while the evening ramp from minimum to peak could exceed 20 GW within a three-hour window. Managing this ramp requires flexible generation, demand response, storage, and — increasingly — imports from neighbouring markets through inter-regional transmission.

Other high-solar markets are experiencing similar challenges. In South Australia, negative wholesale electricity prices (when solar generation exceeds demand and generators pay to keep running) now occur regularly during sunny midday periods. This has driven the emergence of commercial battery storage as an arbitrage asset, charging during negative-price periods and discharging during evening peaks.

The duck curve is not a crisis — it is a solvable engineering and market design challenge — but it does underscore the need for flexibility resources, market reforms that reward flexibility rather than just energy, and investment in inter-regional connections that can absorb surplus renewable generation across wider geographic areas.

### D.2 Frequency Regulation and Inertia

Traditional power systems are stabilised by the mechanical inertia of large rotating generators (turbines and their connected shafts). This inertia acts as a buffer: if a large generator suddenly trips offline, the kinetic energy stored in rotating mass prevents the frequency from collapsing instantly, buying time for backup generators to respond. A power system with high shares of inverter-based generation (solar and wind connected through power electronics) has lower mechanical inertia, making frequency excursions faster and potentially more severe.

System operators in high-renewables markets have developed several approaches to managing reduced inertia:

**Synchronous condensers** — rotating machines disconnected from their mechanical prime mover but still spinning synchronously — can provide synthetic inertia and reactive power support without generating real power. Scotland has deployed several synchronous condensers as its wind share has grown.

**Virtual inertia** from grid-forming inverters — a newer class of inverter control strategy — can emulate the frequency response of a synchronous generator using the energy stored in the DC capacitors or batteries connected to the inverter. Grid-forming inverters are increasingly required by system operators in new renewable projects.

**Fast frequency response (FFR)** from battery storage systems can inject or absorb power within milliseconds of a frequency deviation, faster than any conventional generator. Australia's Hornsdale Power Reserve (a 150 MW / 194 MWh Tesla battery) demonstrated the value of FFR in stabilising the South Australian grid and has been replicated across multiple markets.

### D.3 Reactive Power and Voltage Management

Reactive power — the component of electrical power associated with energy stored in inductors and capacitors rather than consumed — is essential for maintaining voltage within acceptable limits across transmission and distribution networks. Variable renewable energy plants can provide or absorb reactive power through their inverters, but managing reactive power in a highly distributed generation system requires different approaches than in a centralised generation system.

As distributed rooftop solar penetration increases on distribution networks (now exceeding 50 percent of peak load in some Australian distribution zones), the traditional assumption that power flows from the transmission network down to consumers breaks down. Bidirectional power flows on circuits designed for unidirectional flow can cause over-voltage conditions in the middle of sunny days. Solutions include smarter inverter controls (mandating reactive power capability in new inverters), advanced distribution management systems, and battery storage at the distribution level.

## Appendix E: Policy Design Principles for the Clean Energy Transition

### E.1 Technology-Neutral vs. Technology-Specific Support

A longstanding debate in clean energy policy is whether support mechanisms should be technology-neutral (e.g., a carbon price or a clean electricity standard that allows the market to choose the cheapest technology) or technology-specific (e.g., separate auctions for solar, wind, and storage). 

The economic case for technology-neutral policies is strong in principle: allowing competition across technologies tends to drive cost reductions and prevents political capture of support mechanisms. In practice, however, immature technologies often cannot compete against mature ones in neutral mechanisms, even when the immature technologies have significant long-run cost reduction potential. Technology-specific support for offshore wind, green hydrogen, and long-duration storage reflects this reality.

The most robust policy frameworks combine a technology-neutral baseline (typically a carbon price or clean electricity standard) with technology-specific support for early-stage technologies and a targeted industrial policy for strategic supply chains.

### E.2 Auction Design for Renewable Energy

Competitive auctions (also called tenders or reverse auctions) have become the dominant mechanism for procuring renewable energy in most markets. When well-designed, auctions deliver low prices (by intensifying competition), provide investment certainty (through long-term contracts), and enable policy control (over volume, technology mix, and geographic distribution).

Key design parameters:

**Contract type and duration:** Most auctions offer contracts for difference (CfDs), which guarantee a fixed "strike price" and provide payment when market prices fall below that level, while requiring generators to return the difference when market prices exceed it. Typical durations range from 15 to 25 years. Longer contracts reduce financing costs (by reducing revenue uncertainty) but expose governments to long-term price risk.

**Volume and frequency:** Regular, predictable auction schedules allow developers and supply chains to plan investment. Irregular or ad hoc auctions create boom-bust cycles in the supply chain and deter investment in manufacturing capacity.

**Local content requirements:** Some auctions include local content requirements (e.g., minimum percentage of project value sourced domestically). These can support industrial development but risk raising project costs and reducing competition. The WTO has challenged some local content requirements as inconsistent with trade rules.

**Ceiling prices:** Setting an auction ceiling price below market expectations reduces developer participation and can result in no bids being submitted (as has occurred in several UK offshore wind auctions). Ceiling prices should be set with reference to independent cost estimates and reviewed regularly.

### E.3 Carbon Pricing

Carbon pricing — whether through a carbon tax or an emissions trading system (ETS) — provides a market-wide incentive for decarbonisation and generates government revenue that can be recycled into clean energy investment, household support, or deficit reduction. More than 70 carbon pricing instruments are in operation globally, covering approximately 23 percent of global greenhouse gas emissions.

The EU Emissions Trading System (EU ETS) is the world's largest carbon market, covering approximately 40 percent of EU greenhouse gas emissions. EU ETS prices reached a record EUR 100/tonne CO₂ in early 2023 before declining; at current price levels, the ETS provides a meaningful incentive for fuel switching from coal to gas and for investment in energy efficiency, but is insufficient on its own to drive deep decarbonisation without complementary policies.

For carbon pricing to drive renewable energy investment, prices must be credible (investors need confidence that prices will remain high or rise over project lifetimes), predictable (price volatility creates investment uncertainty), and comprehensive (covering the sectors and gases where decarbonisation is needed). Most existing carbon pricing instruments fall short on one or more of these dimensions.
